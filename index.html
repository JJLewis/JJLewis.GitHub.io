A Little Bit About Me

My interest in computers and programming began when I was in Year 7 (2011). I wanted to create something, and at the time, computer and software interested me. I started watching simple Youtube tutorials to learn. From the most basic Hello World iPhone app to a simple calculator app. I think that the only reason that I focus on iOS now is that I found the iPhone Simulator bundled with Xcode cool. From the completion of my first app, my interest snowballed and I began programming in my free time after school. I have started and finished many projects, but there have been some that were over my head at the time. I got my Apple Developer account under my mum's name in Year 8. Since I taught myself how to code, I found later on that I left out many rather quite important things, such as Arrays and Dictionaries... I went back and updated all my apps to be more efficient after I discovered those data types. After I felt comfortable enough in Objective-C (my first language), I played with other languages, in particular, Python and Swift when it came out. Overtime, my way of thinking and programming has changed and gotten much more efficient and readable. I have two large projects that I am very proud of, both of which should be released in the near future.
Recently I have been lucky enough to have been awarded the prestigious Co-op Scholarship for Computer Science to the University of New South Wales. I have commenced my studies in Computer Science now and am set to graduate at the end of 2020.

Decider

My first ever public app.

Decider was the first iOS app that I ever wrote. Back when I was in Year 8, I wrote this app. Back then, my knowledge of algorithms was super close to zero. The only control structures/language functions I new at the time was IF statements, how to assign a text value to a UITextfield, and how to generate random numbers into an int variable. Other than that, all of the GUI was done in a Storyboard file and individual classes where created to match each ViewController. There were literally 1000s of lines of duplicate code and meaningless rubbish at the time. I wish I still had the project file, it’s gone somewhere now, but I’ll be sure to post it when I do find it. It will likely make quite a few developers out there keel over and cry.

The good news is, though, that I completely rewrote the app in Swift, funnily enough, as a way to learn Swift. This time though, I knew how to handle loops, arrays, dictionaries, and other programming language features. At the moment, I fear opening the Swift project because since the Swift syntax has changed since the last time I had opened it. I fear being smacked in the face with tens or even hundreds of little red triangles that tell me XCode can’t compile the project due to syntax issues.

When I first released Decider, all I wanted was about 100 people to pay $0.99 for the app so I could make my membership fees back. Unfortunately things didn’t go so well… So I added banner ads everywhere and made the app free.

Try it out! It should be quite ok/usable now.

DodgeBall

A small step into game development.

Version 2.0

This is a free update to DodgeBall. Unlike a conventional update to an app, this is a complete rewrite of the app from the ground up, making use of Apple’s SpriteKit for the game engine. This provides a much smoother 60 FPS game with nicer animations and infinitely more accurate collision detection. In addition to this, I have removed the old sound effects for better higher quality sound effects.
There is now Game Centre integration so that you can easily compare scores with friends and challenge each other to see who can get the highest score. (Note the score is based on how long you can last without being hit by a red ball.)
There are also score achievements as follows:

  1. Survive 30 Seconds (10 Points)
  2. Survive 45 Seconds (25 Points)
  3. Survive 60 Seconds (50 Points)
  4. Survive 90 Seconds (75 Points)
  5. Survive 120 Seconds (100 Points)

Along with this, there are total game play time achievements as follows:

  1. Play for 15 Minutes (10 Points)
  2. Play for 30 Minutes (25 Points)
  3. Play for 60 Minutes (50 Points)
  4. Play for 24 Hours (100 Points) (I don’t expect anybody to get this one)

Since the game is now free, I can recommend for you to give it a try, it weighs in at 33.9MB *or less, so maybe download it on Wi-Fi.

L120

Wasted potential. Too far ahead of its time.

This was written when I was in Year 10 in 2014, and to be honest, it was 2 years ahead of its time. Transport for NSW had not yet opened to the fact that their paper based logbooks were out of date. Despite taking me 6 months during my free time to create and perfect, it went no where.
The purpose of this app is to fully replace your paper logbook for logging your hours in NSW. It does this by having three main functions:

  1. Automatically Log Drive
  2. Manually Log Drive
  3. Manually Log Lesson

Automatically Log Drive makes use of the GPS function of the phone it is running on to log where the learner is driving and their speed. By doing this, it adds another dimension to the learning experience as the learner can see where they had driven and their speed habits.
Most, if not all functionality that could be automated was automated, this includes:

  • Calculating Day and Night hours based on the day's sunrise and sunset times.
  • Fetching Weather to have a more accurate depiction of the learner's driving conditions.
  • Predictive odometer values based off of the distance travelled in the drive.
  • Entering Supervisor and Vehicle data, by saving presets for them.

This app will soon under go the process of bringing it back up to date from 2 years ago. It will also have a significant UI/UX makeover as I fight the uphill war to get this now approved for official use by learner drivers around the state of New South Wales.

Coming soon to the AppStore.

The Force Project

My greatest creation so far.

The Force Project is my greatest project so far. It is a universal remote for all personal projects, and an awesome tool for people looking to create their own Internet of Things (IoT) devices. It features a super simple to use, user interface designer, so that you can create any interface by using Force's modular tile system. Each tile is a direct implementation of Apple's UIKit, and all attributes are customisable, meaning that the possibilities are endless. Force also has extended tiles, such as Joysticks, MJPEG Stream Viewers, and Myo by Thalmic.
Using Force is as easy as downloading the server script and template, then figuring out how you want to use the data you receive from your user interface that you made. In essence, it is like a universal remote control for all projects.
With Force, my ultimate goal is to empower people to easily create awesome projects and share it with the world. So that more people can discover the fun of creating something and using it or seeing it work.
